# Job Search Plan

A realistic, calibrated job search strategy for an ML practitioner who completes this roadmap by October 2026.

---

## Realistic Hiring Targets

### Who You Will Be After This Roadmap

By October 2026, you will have:
- 4 serious ML books completed with full implementations
- 4 research-grade projects with measurable ARC-AGI benchmarks
- ~85–95 papers read with written summaries
- 1–2 workshop paper submissions
- Demonstrated ability to execute an ambitious 8-month self-directed project

This profile is genuinely unusual. Most job applicants have:
- Online courses without implementation depth
- Kaggle competitions (good signal, but not research-grade)
- No paper reading or writing experience
- Projects that are copies of tutorials

**You will be meaningfully differentiated** — but differentiated does not mean guaranteed.

## Application Strategy

### Where to Apply

1. **ARC-adjacent companies first.** Companies working on reasoning, code generation, symbolic AI, or benchmark-driven research will immediately understand your ARC work.
   - Examples: Harmonic, AlphaCode-adjacent teams, Code Interpreter teams at OpenAI/Anthropic

2. **Companies that value project portfolios.** Startups and research-engineering roles at <200 person companies often hire based on demonstrated work.

3. **Open-source first approach.** Release all 4 projects as high-quality open-source repositories. Being found is better than applying.

4. **Research internships.** If PhD programs interest you, summer research internships at labs (Jan–Mar 2027) are a viable next step.

### How to Present Your Work

- **Lead with the ARC-AGI benchmark score.** "Achieved 55% on ARC-AGI-1, improving on each of 4 projects" is a concrete, impressive, verifiable claim.
- **Lead with the research output.** "Submitted to NeurIPS workshop" is a signal, even if rejected.
- **Show the progression.** Book 1 → Project 1 → Book 2 → Project 2 demonstrates sustained, compounding learning.
- **Quantify everything.** "Read 85 papers" is better than "extensive paper reading."

## LeetCode Strategy (Parallel Prep)

Most ML roles still require coding interviews. This cannot be ignored.

| Month | LeetCode Target | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Mar–May 2026 | 5 problems/week (easy/medium) | Maintain baseline; don't let it atrophy |
| Jun–Aug 2026 | 5 problems/week (medium) | Arrays, graphs, dynamic programming |
| Sep–Oct 2026 | 10 problems/week (medium/hard) | Interview-specific patterns |
| Nov 2026 | Mock interviews 3×/week | Execution under time pressure |

Total: ~180–200 problems. This is sufficient for most ML engineering roles if you already have strong fundamentals.

## Red Flags to Avoid in Applications

- Claiming expertise in areas you cannot demonstrate (avoid: "expert in all things LLMs")
- Listing every tool you've used rather than the ones you've mastered
- Projects without quantitative results ("I built a chatbot" vs "ARC-AGI-1 score: 55%")
- No GitHub activity (commits, stars, issues) — activity signals continued engagement
- Over-relying on the ARC roadmap without connecting it to business/product value in non-research roles

## The PhD Alternative

If the job market disappoints or you want deeper research credentials, a PhD is a viable path after this roadmap. Your ARC work constitutes a research portfolio. Strong PhD application elements:

1. **Statement of Purpose** — your ARC roadmap is a natural narrative arc
2. **Research Output** — workshop paper submission is significant for an applicant without a prior degree
3. **Advisor Targeting** — identify 5–7 professors working on ARC-adjacent topics (abstract reasoning, neurosymbolic AI, program synthesis)
4. **GRE** — Some programs waive it; check program-specific requirements

**Realistic PhD programs given this profile:** Top-30 research universities; competitive for top-15 with a strong paper and recommendation letters.
